Summary of Data Visualisation

Aspects of Data Visualisation

In the 2020 article “Overview of Data Visualisation”, Li explores the different definitions of data visualisation from different perspectives of study, however, they note that in modern times data visualisation comprises of four aspects: namely, real-time interaction, on-the-fly processing, visual scalability and user assistance and personalisation. These four aspects mean that data visualisation must be able to support millions of interactions all whilst remaining in the response time of a few milliseconds, supporting on-the-fly visualisations over sets of raw data, finally, it has user customisation and other user designed features.

Data and Information

Data visualisation is further catagorised into information and scientific visualisation. Information data represents abstract information and scientific information represents scientific information (eg. the human body, the atmosphere, etc). Data itself is catagorised into primary and secondary data. Primary data is collected directly by the researcher, and secondary data is processed primary data for a specific intent. Primary data and secondary data can therefore be thought of as data and information, respectively. Data comprises of entities and relations. Entities refers to objects, and relations refers to the properties of the object. Relations usually do not exist independently. Information, on the other hand, comprises of three parts: information as an organized arrangement of facts or data, information presented as a means of understanding a situation or process, and design of control systems. These three parts determine how easily information can be extracted, how information relates to certain subjects or scenarios, and the feedback that a product gives. Li then goes on to give examples of common information and scientific visualisations – tables, charts, tress maps and simulations, volume, waveforms respectively. Overall, all these traditional types of data visualisation have characteristics of readability, recognizability and meaning, amongst others.

Human Interpretation and Understanding

Data visualisation relies on human perception and understanding, which means that data a visualisation effectiveness is determined by how well a particular person can interpret it. Li mentions Haeley’s 2007 theories of preattentive processing.

Feature integration theory

Developed by Anne Treisman and Garry Gelade, this theory suggests human perceives a stimulus, features are “registered early, automatically, and in parallel, while objects are identified separately”. It was made to determine which properties are detected preatentively, and how the human brain performs this process.

Texton theory

Developed by Bela Julész, to study texture patterns and if features are detected by the human visual system. He determined that there are three textons. Elongated blobs: specific properties such as hue, orientation, and width. For example, line segments, rectangles, ellipses; terminators: ends of line segments; crossings of line segments. It is believed that only a difference in textons or in their density can be detected preattentively.

Similarity theory

This theory focuses on search time for perception. Search time depends on the amount of information needed to identify an object and how easily that object is distinguished from distractors.

Guided search theory

Made by JeremyWolfe, it is a two-stage model of visual processing in which initial parallel search mechanisms direct subsequent serial search mechanisms.

Thoughts

Although the main summarized text by Li primarily focuses on quantitative data, there is need to know how one might record or visualise qualitative data. Nelson (2006), suggests that this type of knowledge (qualitative) is knowledge by acquaintance and knowledge of how to, whereas quantitative knowledge is factual and “is typically articulated in a set of testable and falsifiable propositions” (Nelson, 2006). Nelson suggests ways in which one can access qualitative knowledge. In particular, he speaks about practice-as-research. This is used when words are unable to communicate the knowledge, however, workshop education affords insights into data. For example, you may be unable to describe to someone your process of riding a bicycle and how you keep your balance, however, as the researcher attempts to ride the bicycle on their own, they are able to learn and understand how to keep their balance. Qualitative data may further serve as evidence, for example, an eye witness statement or a survey about someone’s experiences.
